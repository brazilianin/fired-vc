The First-Mover Window

Tools get replaced. Companions do not.

Every previous platform shift created tools. Companies adopted them, then swapped them for whatever shipped next quarter. Agentic AI is the first shift in computing history that creates companions — systems that learn the operator, accumulate context, and become harder to remove the longer they run. The switching cost compounds. That is a moat no SaaS company ever had.

The cloud wave — 2006–2016

  • AWS, Stripe, Twilio: built on $1–3M seed checks
  • Time-to-IPO compressed from 12 yrs to 7
  • Seed VCs who deployed in 2008–12 captured ~25–40% IRR over the decade
  • USV Fund I (2004, $125M) → estimated 8–9x net, lifetime carry north of $1B

The mobile wave — 2010–2018

  • Uber, Snap, Instagram, Slack: app-distribution made networks effortless
  • $5M seed routinely became $100M Series A in 18 months
  • Andreessen Horowitz Fund I (2009, $300M) → 6.6x
  • SV Angel’s 2010–12 vintage outperformed the S&P by 12x

The agentic wave — 2025→

  • One operator + agents = revenue in weeks, not years
  • Capital required drops another order of magnitude ($50–150K, not $5M)
  • Network effects are data + expertise — unforgeable, not just sticky
  • The vintage of agentic seed checks written in 2025–2027 is the one that will produce this cycle’s USV / a16z

Speed is the entire moat.

In a cloud or mobile wave, a 12-month head start was a real lead. In an agentic wave, a 12-week head start compounds into a permanent data advantage. The operator who ships agentic in Q1 has a year of proprietary workflow data by Q4 that no competitor can backfill without a year of operating at the same volume. Fund I deploys now — not after the playbooks are written.

The next decade of software gives attention back.

Why Toronto

Where world-class AI talent, applied industry problems, immigrant founder energy, and capital-efficient building meet — with the U.S. next door.

We are not arguing Toronto is the next San Francisco. We are arguing it is one of the best places in the world to build an AI-native company in 2026: deep research roots, real applied-AI density, materially lower build costs, and a clean Canada→U.S. scale pathway.

The deep-learning roots are real.

Modern deep learning was built in Canada. Geoffrey Hinton at the University of Toronto, Yoshua Bengio in Montréal, and Yann LeCun shared the 2018 ACM Turing Award — computing’s Nobel — for the breakthroughs that made neural networks the foundation of modern AI. Canada then launched the Pan-Canadian AI Strategy in 2017, the world’s first national AI strategy, anchoring three federally-backed institutes: Vector (Toronto), Mila (Montréal), and Amii (Edmonton).

Vector Institute is Toronto’s AI anchor.

Vector is not a university lab — it is an independent institute that gives Toronto an institutional centre of gravity: faculty, graduate students, corporate partners, startup networks, applied research, and talent programs all orbit around it. Its stated core research areas are Health AI, Trustworthy AI, ML fundamentals, and Generative Models.

The research being done here is world-leading.

Talent is cheaper to assemble

The tax structure rewards builders

Canada’s SR&ED program returns up to 35% of qualifying R&D as a refundable credit. Stack with Ontario co-credits and the USD→CAD currency advantage — effective build cost drops materially.

Capital-efficient by culture

Less local late-stage VC density historically forced Canadian founders to validate revenue earlier than peers in SF or Boston. That trait is suddenly an asset in the agentic era — when small teams need to reach product and revenue milestones before raising large rounds.

Canada Startup Visa approvals went from 1,070 in 2022 → 5,595 in 2024. In the 48 hours after one high-profile U.S. policy shift, 10,000+ U.S.-based tech workers applied to move north.

What Toronto isn’t

Toronto isn’t San Francisco. Late-stage AI capital, hyperscaler proximity, repeat-AI-founder density, and the speed of enterprise customer adoption still concentrate south of the border. That’s exactly why fired.vc is structured as a cross-border studio: we incubate inside Toronto’s research and talent density, then plug each portfolio company into U.S. customers and follow-on capital through Sunil’s Techstars and FI networks. Toronto is the bridge — not the destination.
